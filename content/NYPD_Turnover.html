Predicting Turnover Rate for NYPD¶

Using NYC OpenData and Crime Statistics¶

By Bethan Cordone and Itzel Melgoza¶

Introduction¶

Turnover rate refers to the rate at which employees leave a company or organization in a specified interval of time. A high turnover rate can prove costly to the company or institution as it will entail more frequent training of incoming workers. The NYC Citywide Payroll Data published on NYC OpenData contains payroll information for all municipal workers from the years 2014 to 2021. Given that the data provides information on an employee's start date as well as whether they are still actively working with an agency, we aimed to use this dataset to predict whether someone will stop working for an agency after 2 years. We were particularly interested in the effect of work location borough on predicting whether an employee stays on for more than 2 years. We decided to focus on the police department. Police departments characteristically have relatively low turnover rates with a national total turnover rate of 11% in the early 2000s (Wareham, 2015). Turnover rates were found to be highest in municipal agencies and rural areas. Through our data exploration, we explored how turnover rates in New York compare to the national average. In this project, we aim to predict whether someone will stop working for the police department after 2 years. We also explored patterns associated with our predictor variables and turnover rates.

We also created new features for our data set in an attempt to find new patterns and better predict turnover rates. We added features relating to crime rates and a feature relating to the percentage of leadership positions out of total positions. These attempted to answer the question of whether increasing crime rates affected turnover and if the percentage of leadership positions affected turnover.

Additional 'Police Department' Agency¶

Upon further investigation into the agencies, we discovered that there was another agency labeled as "Police Department". We decided to join this agency to the larger "POLICE DEPARTMENT" agency and perform EDA analysis to determine what data to include in the model. Our EDA process for the joined data is described below; we chose to remove the code as we ultimetely decided to only use the 'POLICE DEPARTMENT' data in our model.

The "Police Department" data only records data for the year 2014 while the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" data includes the years 2015-2021 and also includes borough information. The 2014 dataset does not include borough information. Based off of this exploration, we had two options for moving forward: we could have a year range of 2014-2021 and introduce data that does not have borough information or we could limit the year range to 2015-2021 and keep data that only has borough information. We used our EDA of the joined police departments and the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" agency alone to determine what data to include in the model. We also compared the proportion of employees that stop working after 2 years when using the joined police department subset and the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" agency alone.

The joined police department data is similar to the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" data alone in that the top ten positions account for 81% of the records for the agency and similar differences in base salary across title descriptions were observed. There are 861 employees in the smaller police department that are not in the larger police department subset.There are employees that are not included in the larger "POLICE DEPARTMENT" dataset. We can not know for certain the leave status of these employees after the year 2014. Therefore, this issue, along with the fact that records in the "Police Department" agency do not have information on boroughs makes this data less desirable for inclusion in the model.

We joined the datsets so that an extra year was represented and changed the missing borough values to "UNKNOWN". We first decided to look at the distribution of base salary. We examined the same viola and borough scatter plots as for 'POLICE DEPARTMENT' alone and determined that the plots were all similar. We examined the collinearity and observed the same features displaying collinearity as in the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" subset alone. When we included the "Police Department" Agency, the proportion of individuals who stopped working after 2 year was 4.19% and now without it the proportion of individuals is 4.62%.

The difference in proportions seems to suggest that there is not a significant change in proportion when we don't include the "Police Department" Agency. We also established earlier that the EDA of the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" records and the EDA of the joined police department records yielded similar results. As we intend to introduce external data that is specific to boroughs, we decided that it would be appropriate to proceed with using the records from the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" agency only for initial algorithm development.

Total Years Column¶

In order to determine if someone leaves the agency after two years, we decided to first find the number of total years they have worked. We are particularly interested in the turnover rate per borough as the movement of one person from one borough to another could lead to management having to find a replacement for an employee and contribute to training costs. As we're interested in comparing turnover rates between boroughs, we decided to group records by both names and borough. This is because there are employees who who have worked in multiple boroughs.

In some instances, an individual has multiple start years for the same borough. An example is an employee that first started in Manhattan in 2016 and was ceased in 2017. She then started in Queens in 2018 and is listed as still active there despite the data also showing that she started in Manhattan again in 2019 and has since been ceased in Manhattan. In order to deal with data quality issues such as these, we decided to take the minimum start year per borough. While this assumption ignores that she seemingly joined the Manhattan borough again, this assumption was necessary to be consistent with the fact that she is still active in Queens. This example employee has different total years worked per borough which is the desired outcome given our aim of exploring how turnover rate can vary between borough.

Introducing New Features¶

Crime counts per borough per year (external data)¶

We wanted to explore whether increasing crime rates had an effect on turnover rate among police officers. We were able to find a data set from the NYC open data that had records of crime incidents ranging from the 1920s to 2021. There are 449,506 incidents recorded with 36 variables describing each incident.

We wanted to create new features that would give the number of incidents of crime for each year and borough. We also chose to break the crime incidents into different categories as we thought that may be of additional significance to our posed question. For instance, it seems reasonable that theoretically increasing felony crimes would have an affect on police turnover while violations did not have an effect or as strong an effect. We used the recorded start date of the incident to categorize the year of the crime, the borough name to determine its location, and the law category variable to determine the type of crime. The law category variable broke the crimes into felonies, misdemeanors, and violations. We could have categorized the crimes in another manner, for instance violent and non-violent but this process seemed to require us to make our own assumptions and we wanted to avoid this.

The crime instances of each type were grouped and counted by the year and borough. The result was 3 new features that give the number of incidents of crime of the 3 types for each year and borough.

We decided to use logistic regression to predict churn as the problem we are trying to solve is an issue of classification. We are classifying records of a city employee's salary per fiscal year, given a specific borough, as either belonging to one of two classes. Given that we have a lot more observations than features, we were able to proceed with using logistic regression. The limitation of logistic regression is that we established earlier that there is collinearity in the dataset. Our results did not seem to agree with the article we read that indicated increasing crime was linked to early resignation and retirement. It is possible that if turnover was defined differently, for instance looking at people who left after 8 years, that this trend would be observed. The work locations of Brooklyn and Queens also decreased the odds of turnover. Additionally, the strongest negative coefficient was that of the percentage of leadership positions. It could be that the opportunity for promotion highly persuades individuals to stay on with a borough department. Interestingly, Manhattan had the highest proportion of leadership positions but being in Manhattan also increased the odds of turnover. The current date we have does not seem to explain this contradiction, but there may be external factors related to borough-specific demographics.

Conclusion¶

The aim of this project was to determine how well can we predict if someone will stop working for an agency after 2 years. After subsetting the data to focus on the "POLICE DEPARTMENT" agency, we classified records of data as either belonging to a person who stopped working in a certain borough after or before 2 years. It was necessary to group records by both name and borough as the same individual could work at different borough locations. To see how well we could predict classification, we compared the performance of a logistic regression model to the base rate of natural turnover. After finding that the model performed better than predicting randomly, we adjusted for collinearity and used the model to explore patterns. We found some expected patterns including that a greater proportion of leadership positions decreased the likelihood of turnover. We also found a lot of surprising patterns including that being located in Manhattan and Richmond, areas with higher mean base salaries, increased the odds of turnover. We also had expected crime counts to have a larger effect, but this could have resulted from only focusing on individuals that displayed a 2 year turnover. In regard to limitations of this analysis, we believe that the proportion of turnover could have been inflated as our methods led to individuals who were not ceased and who started in 2019 or after as being categorized as having turned over within 2 years. In regard to future directions, turnover in the police department will be critical to understand as police officers are reporting low morale and departments are attempting to attract and retain employees.
